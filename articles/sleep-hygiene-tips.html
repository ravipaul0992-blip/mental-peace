10 Sleep Hygiene Tips for a Restful Night

May 10, 20265 min read

Sleep and anxiety have a complicated relationship. Anxiety makes it hard to sleep, and poor sleep makes anxiety worse. Breaking this cycle starts with sleep hygiene: the habits and environment that set you up for quality rest.

You don't need to overhaul your entire life. Even adopting two or three of these tips can make a meaningful difference.

1. Keep a Consistent Schedule

Go to bed and wake up at the same time every day, even on weekends. Your body's internal clock (circadian rhythm) thrives on consistency. Irregular sleep schedules confuse your brain about when it should be alert and when it should rest.

2. Create a Wind-Down Routine

Give yourself 30-60 minutes of transition time before bed. Your brain can't switch from high-speed to sleep mode instantly. Try reading, gentle stretching, a warm bath, or our breathing exercises.

3. Make Your Bedroom a Sleep Sanctuary

  • Cool: 65-68 degrees F (18-20 degrees C) is optimal
  • Dark: Use blackout curtains or an eye mask
  • Quiet: Earplugs or a white noise machine help
  • Clean: A tidy space reduces mental clutter
  • Bed = sleep: Avoid working, eating, or scrolling in bed

4. Limit Screen Time Before Bed

Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production. Try to stop using screens 30-60 minutes before bed. If you must use your phone, enable the blue light filter/night mode.

5. Watch Your Caffeine

Caffeine has a half-life of about 5-6 hours, meaning half of your 3 PM coffee is still in your system at 9 PM. Try to have your last caffeinated drink before 2 PM, especially if you're sensitive to caffeine.

6. Move Your Body (But Not Too Late)

Regular exercise improves sleep quality significantly. However, vigorous exercise within 2-3 hours of bedtime can be stimulating. Aim for morning or afternoon activity, and keep evening movement gentle.

7. Be Mindful of Alcohol

While alcohol might help you fall asleep faster, it disrupts sleep quality, particularly REM sleep (the restorative stage). It also increases nighttime awakenings and can worsen anxiety the next day.

8. Use the 20-Minute Rule

If you've been lying in bed for more than 20 minutes and can't sleep, get up. Go to another room and do something calm (read, listen to soft music) until you feel sleepy. Lying awake in bed trains your brain to associate bed with wakefulness.

9. Try a Gratitude Practice

Before closing your eyes, mentally list three good things from your day, no matter how small. This redirects your brain from worry to appreciation and has been shown to improve both sleep quality and duration.

10. Limit Naps

If you nap, keep it under 20 minutes and before 3 PM. Long or late naps can interfere with nighttime sleep. A "coffee nap" (drink coffee, then nap for 15 minutes) can be an effective afternoon energy boost without disrupting nighttime sleep.

Start Tonight

Pick just 2-3 tips from this list and try them tonight. Small, consistent changes compound over time. Within a week, you may notice your sleep improving. Within a month, it can be transformative.

Sleep is not a luxury. It's a biological necessity and a form of self-care. Prioritizing sleep is one of the most powerful things you can do for your mental health.
