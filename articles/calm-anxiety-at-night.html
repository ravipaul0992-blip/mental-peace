How to Calm Anxiety at Night: 7 Techniques That Actually Work

May 23, 2026 6 min read

The house is quiet. The lights are off. And suddenly, your mind decides it's the perfect time to review every mistake you've ever made, every problem that needs solving, and every worst-case scenario imaginable. Sound familiar?

Nighttime anxiety is incredibly common. When the distractions of the day fade away, your mind fills the silence with worry. But you don't have to lie there suffering. These seven techniques can help you reclaim your nights.

Why Anxiety Gets Worse at Night

During the day, your brain is occupied with tasks, conversations, and activities. At night, without these distractions, your mind has space to wander, and it often wanders toward worry. Additionally, fatigue lowers your emotional resilience, making anxious thoughts feel bigger and more overwhelming than they would during the day.

1. The Brain Dump Journal

Keep a notebook by your bed. Before you try to sleep, spend 5-10 minutes writing down everything on your mind: worries, to-do lists, random thoughts, all of it. Think of it as transferring your worries from your brain to the paper.

Try This Tonight

Write at the top of the page: "Things I can deal with tomorrow." Then list everything that's weighing on you. Give yourself permission to let them go until morning.

2. The 4-7-8 Breathing Technique

This technique, developed by Dr. Andrew Weil, acts as a natural tranquilizer for your nervous system:

  • Breathe in through your nose for 4 seconds
  • Hold your breath for 7 seconds
  • Exhale slowly through your mouth for 8 seconds
  • Repeat 4 times

The extended exhale activates your parasympathetic nervous system, signaling your body that it's safe to relax. Try our interactive breathing tool to practice this technique.

3. Progressive Muscle Relaxation

Anxiety stores itself in your muscles. This technique systematically releases that tension:

  • Starting with your toes, tense each muscle group for 5 seconds
  • Then release completely for 10 seconds
  • Work your way up: feet, calves, thighs, stomach, chest, hands, arms, shoulders, face
  • Notice the contrast between tension and relaxation

By the time you reach your face, your entire body will feel noticeably more relaxed.

4. The "Worry Window" Strategy

Instead of fighting nighttime worries, schedule a specific "worry time" earlier in the evening, perhaps 15 minutes after dinner. During this window, worry as much as you want. Write down concerns and possible solutions.

Then, when worries arise at bedtime, remind yourself: "I've already addressed this during my worry window. I can revisit tomorrow if needed." Over time, your brain learns that bedtime is not for worrying.

5. Create a Sensory Cocoon

Design your bedroom environment to signal safety to your nervous system:

  • Temperature: Keep your room cool (65-68 degrees F is ideal)
  • Darkness: Use blackout curtains or an eye mask
  • Sound: Try white noise, rain sounds, or gentle ambient music
  • Scent: Lavender has been shown to reduce anxiety. A few drops on your pillow can help
  • Touch: A weighted blanket can provide a sense of security

6. The "Cognitive Shuffle"

This clever technique was developed by cognitive scientist Luc Beaudoin. It works by giving your mind something to do that's engaging enough to prevent worry, but boring enough to induce sleep:

  • Pick a random letter (like "B")
  • Think of words starting with that letter, visualizing each one: beach, banana, butterfly, book, bridge...
  • Don't rush. Spend a few seconds picturing each item
  • If you run out, pick another letter

This exercise mimics the random, associative thinking that happens as you drift off to sleep, essentially tricking your brain into sleep mode.

7. The "Worst Case, Best Case, Most Likely" Exercise

When a specific worry won't let go, challenge it with perspective:

  • Worst case: What's the absolute worst that could happen? (Usually not as catastrophic as anxiety suggests)
  • Best case: What's the best possible outcome?
  • Most likely: What will probably actually happen? (Usually somewhere in the middle and quite manageable)

This exercise helps your rational mind regain control from the anxious, catastrophizing part of your brain.

Your bed should be a sanctuary, not a battlefield. With practice, you can teach your mind that nighttime is for rest, not rehearsal of worries.

When to Seek Additional Help

If nighttime anxiety is a regular occurrence that significantly impacts your sleep and daily functioning, consider speaking with a healthcare professional. Chronic sleep disruption from anxiety can affect every area of your life, and you deserve support.

Your Nighttime Anxiety Toolkit

Tonight, try just one of these techniques. Don't pressure yourself to do them all. Pick the one that resonates most, and give it an honest try for a week. Small, consistent steps lead to meaningful change.
